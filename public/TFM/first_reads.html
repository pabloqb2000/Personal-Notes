How to Pseudo-CT: A Comparative Review of Deep Convolutional Neural Network Architectures for CT Synthesis

[LocalRef] [Paper]

Compared methods

  1. U-Net
  2. Atrous-Net
  3. Residual-Net

Datasets

  1. Pairs of 3D T1-weighted MR and Low-dose CT images from the head of 19 healthy women
  2. Dual echo Dixon-VIBE MR images and CT images from the pelvis of 13 colorectal and 6 prostate cancer patients Dixon sequences include 4 images: water, fat, in phase and out-of-phase

Introduction

[1]

Older methods include non-machine-learning approaches, but they have lower accuracy and longer inference times.
Newer methods propose U-Net architectures, DL segmentation, dilated convolutions, residual connections and auto-context refinement, adversarial NN,...
This paper compares the proposed methods in both datasets using a 2D and a 3D version of each method.

  1. 2D schemes use 2D slices from the MR data to generate the 2D slices of the Pseudo-CT
  2. 3D schemes on the other hand use 3D patches along with 3D convolutions to generate the Pseudo-CT 3D patches.
Dixon images are fed as 4-channel images

Conclusions

[5]

The 3D scheme works better if the bone presents complex 3D structures across the slices. The loss of context information for the use of 3D patches is compensated by a smoother bone depiction in the result. Instead, if the bone does not vary across slices, it is better to use a slice-by-slice strategy with 2D filters.

The best models for each scheme were:

  1. 3D U-Nets: for 3D schemes
  2. 2D Residual networks: for 2D schemes
Thus, for the head images the best model were 3D U-Nets, while 2D Residual networks performed better for the pelvic images.

The Dixon-Vibe MRI is the usual sequence acquired in the clinic. However, it probably does not contain enough information for pseudo-CT generation, given the similar results between the evaluated networks and schemes. Hence, in future works it would be interesting to compare results with Dixon-VIBE, T1 or even the recent zero-echo-time acquisitions.
